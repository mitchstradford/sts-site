CDTFA Cannabis Audits: What Dispensaries Need to Know

California cannabis businesses face some of the most complex tax obligations in the state — and the CDTFA audits the industry aggressively.

California cannabis businesses operate under one of the most complex and heavily scrutinized tax environments in the country. Between the CDTFA's cannabis excise tax, state and local sales tax, the ongoing effects of federal tax law, and the heightened audit focus on the industry, staying compliant requires specialized knowledge that goes well beyond general tax experience.

The California cannabis tax landscape

Cannabis excise tax

California imposes a cannabis excise tax on retail cannabis sales. As of 2023, the excise tax is collected directly at retail, at a rate of 15% of the gross receipts from the retail sale. This excise tax is administered by the CDTFA separately from sales tax, and maintaining accurate records of retail sales, returns, and excise tax remittances is essential.

State sales tax

Cannabis products sold at retail are subject to California's standard sales tax (currently 7.25% statewide, plus applicable district taxes). Cannabis is not treated as a food item — all retail cannabis sales are taxable.

Local cannabis taxes

Many California cities and counties impose additional local cannabis taxes — often calculated on gross receipts, weight, or units sold. The combination of excise tax, state sales tax, and local cannabis taxes can result in effective tax burdens exceeding 35–40% of retail price in high-tax jurisdictions.

Why the CDTFA audits cannabis businesses aggressively

Cannabis is a cash-heavy, newly regulated industry with a significant history of unlicensed operations. Key audit triggers include heavy cash handling (due to limited banking access), the complexity of multiple overlapping taxes, and California's METRC track-and-trace system — which records cannabis product movement from cultivation through retail and allows the CDTFA to compare track-and-trace data directly against reported sales figures.

What CDTFA auditors look at in a cannabis audit

Reconciling METRC data to reported sales

The METRC system records every tagged cannabis package through its retail sale. Auditors will pull this data and compare it to your reported gross receipts. Discrepancies — even those explained by waste, returns, promotional samples, or theft — need to be documented.

Excise tax collection and remittance

Auditors verify that excise tax was collected on all retail sales and remitted correctly. Errors in excise tax calculation — including applying the wrong rate, failing to collect on certain product categories, or reconciliation errors between reported retail sales and excise tax filings — are common findings.

Point-of-sale system data

Your dispensary POS system (BLAZE, Flowhub, Dutchie, etc.) is a key audit resource. Auditors will request POS reports for the audit period and reconcile them against your filed returns. Discrepancies between POS data, METRC records, and CDTFA filings raise significant red flags.

Bank deposit analysis

Even for cash-heavy businesses, most cannabis operations maintain some banking relationship. Auditors will request bank statements and reconcile deposits against reported gross receipts.

Complimentary products and promotional items

California generally treats promotional cannabis given to customers as a taxable retail sale at the fair market value of the product — meaning you may owe excise tax and sales tax on items given away for free. Documentation of what was given away, to whom, and under what circumstances is important.

Common compliance failures in California cannabis

  • Failure to collect excise tax on all retail transactions
  • Excise tax calculation errors (using incorrect rate or gross receipt amounts)
  • Inconsistent treatment of returns, waste, and promotional items
  • METRC discrepancies that don't have supporting documentation
  • Failure to file or remit when due because of cash flow pressures
  • Inadequate separation of excise tax records from sales tax records

What to do if you receive a CDTFA audit notice

If your cannabis business receives a CDTFA audit notice, the stakes are high and the complexity is real. The industry-specific elements — excise tax, METRC, multi-layer taxation — require a representative with specific experience in California cannabis taxation. Do not attempt to manage a cannabis audit without qualified representation.

The bottom line

California cannabis businesses face some of the most demanding tax compliance requirements of any industry in the state. CDTFA audits of cannabis businesses are detailed, data-driven, and consequential. The best defense is proactive compliance — accurate record-keeping, proper use of your track-and-trace system, and regular internal reconciliation of your CDTFA obligations.
