Sales Tax for California Contractors and Construction Companies

Construction sales tax in California involves both taxable materials and exempt labor — and the rules for separating them are easy to misapply.

Construction sales tax in California is genuinely complicated — complicated enough that even experienced contractors and their accountants frequently get it wrong. The core issue is that construction jobs involve both taxable materials and exempt labor, and California's rules for separating them are specific, fact-dependent, and easy to misapply.

The CDTFA audits contractors regularly, and construction-related errors are among the most common — and most expensive — that businesses in this space make.

The basic framework: contractors as consumers of materials

In California, contractors generally occupy a unique position in the sales tax system. Unlike retailers (who collect tax from customers), contractors are typically treated as the consumer of the materials they incorporate into real property. This means contractors pay sales tax (or use tax) when they purchase materials — not when they bill the customer — and the charge to the customer for a construction contract is generally not subject to sales tax.

Lump-sum contracts vs. time-and-material contracts

Lump-sum contracts

In a lump-sum contract, you charge the customer a single price for the entire job. For sales tax purposes, you pay tax on your material purchases, and the lump-sum charge to the customer is not taxable. The risk: if you bought materials without paying sales tax (e.g., using a resale certificate incorrectly), you now owe use tax on those materials at their purchase cost.

Time-and-material contracts

In a time-and-material contract, you separately bill the customer for materials (often at a markup) and labor. If materials are separately stated and billed at a price higher than your cost, the CDTFA may treat you as a retailer of those materials — meaning you should have collected sales tax from the customer on the material portion of the invoice. Getting this wrong creates audit exposure in either direction.

Fixtures vs. materials: another key distinction

California distinguishes between materials (items that become part of the real property and lose their identity) and fixtures (items that remain tangible personal property even after installation, such as certain equipment or appliances).

  • Materials incorporated into real property: Contractor pays tax at purchase; no tax on the customer billing
  • Fixtures: Generally treated as a retail sale from contractor to customer — tax should be collected from the customer on the selling price

Common fixture examples include commercial kitchen equipment, HVAC units, certain lighting fixtures, and built-in appliances. Whether something is a fixture or a material is a facts-and-circumstances determination that contractors and auditors frequently disagree on.

Subcontractor relationships

When a general contractor hires subcontractors, the tax treatment of those relationships requires attention. Payments to subcontractors for labor are not subject to sales tax. Payments that include materials may require analysis of the subcontractor's tax status. Proper documentation of subcontractor agreements — what's included in each payment, who is providing materials and who is providing labor — is essential for a clean audit.

Common CDTFA audit findings for contractors

  • Untaxed material purchases — the most common finding: materials purchased without paying sales tax, with no corresponding use tax reported on the CDTFA return
  • Incorrect fixture vs. material classification
  • Improper use of resale certificates
  • Time-and-material billing treated incorrectly, failing to collect tax on the material portion
  • Subcontractor documentation gaps

Steps to reduce your audit risk

  • Review your contract templates — the language you use directly affects your tax treatment
  • Maintain purchase records organized by job so you can document what materials went into each project
  • Understand when your contract structure puts you in retailer status for materials
  • Reconcile your material purchases against use tax reported on your returns at least annually

The bottom line

Construction is one of the most sales-tax-complex industries in California, and CDTFA auditors are experienced at finding the specific issues that arise in this space. With proper contract structuring and record-keeping, most common exposures are manageable.
