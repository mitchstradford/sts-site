Sales Tax Audits for Restaurants in California

Restaurants are among the most frequently audited businesses in California. Here's what you need to know to protect yours.

Restaurants are among the most frequently audited businesses in California. The CDTFA targets food service operations for good reason: the industry involves complex taxability rules, high transaction volumes, heavy cash handling, and a long history of underreporting — whether intentional or not.

If you own or operate a restaurant in California, understanding why audits happen and how they work is the first step toward protecting your business.

Why restaurants get audited

The CDTFA uses several indicators to flag restaurants for audit. A low effective tax rate compared to industry benchmarks is a primary trigger. The CDTFA also uses markup analysis — taking your cost of goods and applying standard food service markups to estimate what your sales should have been. Cash-heavy operations, discrepancies between sales and purchases, and prior audit history all increase your chances of selection.

What California restaurant sales tax rules look like

California's food taxability rules are unusually complex. The basic framework:

  • Exempt: Food sold for off-premises consumption that is not hot, not carbonated, and not candy or confection — such as grocery-type items.
  • Taxable: Food sold hot, food sold for on-premises consumption, soft drinks and carbonated beverages.
  • The 80/80 rule: If more than 80% of a restaurant's revenue comes from food sales AND more than 80% of food sales are taxable, then cold food items that would otherwise be exempt become taxable at that location if adequate documentation is not maintained to support the exempt nature of the transactions. This rule catches many restaurants off guard.
  • 3rd Party Delivery Services: Different 3rd party delivery services treat sales tax differently. Some 3rd party delivery services retain and remit sales tax to CDTFA, where as other 3rd party delivery services do not retain the sales tax and it is the restaurant's responisbility to report the taxable sales and remit the tax associated with those sales.
  • Caterers: Caterers have specific rules that apply to venue rental, the provision of meals, and the variety of services that often accompany special events.

How CDTFA auditors examine restaurants

Markup analysis

The auditor will request your food and beverage purchase records and use published or industry-standard markup ratios to estimate what your sales should have been. If your reported sales fall significantly short of the estimate, the auditor will likely use this markup method to calculate an assessed deficiency. The markup method is an estimate, not a precise calculation — the ratios used, the categories of purchases included, and the assumed waste and comping rates all affect the result and can often be challenged.

POS and Z-tape analysis

Auditors will request point-of-sale data and compare it against filed returns. If your POS data doesn't clearly separate taxable and exempt sales (hot vs. cold, eat-in vs. take-out), the auditor may treat a larger portion of your sales as taxable.

Bank deposit analysis

Auditors often reconcile your reported gross sales against bank deposits. Deposits that significantly exceed reported sales suggest unreported revenue. Auditors often will evaluate the percentage of sales that were made via credit card or debit card in order to determine if all the cash sales were properly reported.

Comps, voids, and employee meals

Complimentary meals, employee meals, and voided transactions are areas of scrutiny. Comped and voided sales need to be properly documented — auditors may add them back if records aren't clear. Employee meals are generally taxable at the retail value unless certain conditions apply.

Common audit findings in restaurant cases

  • Failure to apply tax to hot food, drinks, or to-go orders that should have been taxed
  • Incorrect application of the 80/80 rule
  • Unreported cash sales
  • Inadequate documentation for exempt sales (catering, wholesale)
  • Inconsistent tip and service charge treatment
  • Inadequate records for comps and voids

How to reduce your audit risk

  • Invest in a properly configured POS system that categorizes every item as taxable or exempt and separately tracks eat-in vs. take-out
  • Document everything — comps, employee meals, catering contracts, void reasons, and daily close reports — for at least four years
  • Reconcile your returns before filing — compare reported gross sales and taxable sales against your POS data and bank deposits
  • Understand whether the 80/80 rule applies to your operation

If you're already under audit

Restaurant audits are defensible — even when they've already begun. The markup method produces estimates, not facts, and those estimates can often be reduced by presenting alternative data, challenging the markup ratios used, or documenting categories of sales the auditor excluded. Early representation matters most.

The bottom line

California restaurant sales tax is genuinely complicated, and the CDTFA audits the industry aggressively. That doesn't mean an audit will result in a large assessment — it means preparation, documentation, and representation matter more for restaurants than for most other business types.
