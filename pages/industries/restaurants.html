Restaurants & Food Service

California restaurants face some of the most complex sales tax rules in the state — and the CDTFA audits the industry aggressively. We help food service businesses defend audits, fix compliance gaps, and recover overpayments.

Why restaurants are a top CDTFA audit target

The CDTFA targets restaurants for a simple reason: the industry combines high transaction volumes, heavy cash handling, and genuinely complicated taxability rules. Auditors have well-established methodologies specifically designed for food service businesses — including the markup method, POS analysis, and bank deposit reconciliation — and they apply them routinely.

Being audited doesn't mean you've done something wrong. It does mean that how you respond — and how well your records support your positions — matters enormously.

California food taxability rules for restaurants

California's food sales tax rules are unusually complex. The basic framework:

  • Exempt: Unprepared food sold for off-premises consumption — cold items sold for take-home like grocery-type products.
  • Taxable: Hot prepared food, food sold for on-premises consumption, carbonated beverages, and most candy and confections.
  • The 80/80 rule: If more than 80% of your revenue comes from food AND more than 80% of food sales are taxable, then cold to-go items that would otherwise be exempt become taxable. This rule catches many restaurants off guard.
  • Beverages: Hot coffee and tea are taxable. Iced coffee and iced tea are not. Carbonated drinks are always taxable.
  • Delivery platforms: Sales through DoorDash, Uber Eats, and similar platforms raise additional questions about tax collection and remittance that require careful reconciliation.

How CDTFA auditors examine restaurants

A typical restaurant audit involves the following:

  • Markup analysis. Auditors take your food and beverage purchase records and apply industry-standard markup ratios to estimate what your sales should have been. If your reported sales fall short, the difference becomes an assessed deficiency. These ratios are estimates and can often be successfully challenged.
  • POS analysis. Auditors compare your point-of-sale data against filed returns. If your POS doesn't clearly separate taxable and exempt sales, the auditor may treat a larger share of your sales as taxable.
  • Bank deposit reconciliation. Reported gross sales are compared against bank deposits. Unexplained gaps suggest unreported revenue.
  • Comps, voids, and employee meals. These are scrutinized closely. Undocumented comps and voids may be added back to taxable sales.

Common issues we find — and fix

  • POS systems incorrectly categorizing taxable vs. exempt items
  • Failure to apply (or incorrect application of) the 80/80 rule
  • Inadequate documentation for comps, voids, and employee meals
  • Inconsistent treatment of service charges and automatic gratuities
  • Third-party delivery platform reconciliation errors
  • Overpayment of sales tax on cold, exempt food items
  • Missing records for catering contracts or wholesale sales

How we help restaurants

  • Audit defense. We manage every aspect of your CDTFA audit — from the initial notice through closing. We challenge flawed markup methodologies, document your exempt sales, and negotiate the assessment.
  • Audit appeals. If an audit produces an assessment you believe is wrong, we build and argue your appeal at the CDTFA Appeals Bureau and the Office of Tax Appeals.
  • Refund recovery. Many restaurants have overpaid sales tax on exempt food items for years without knowing it. We identify overpayments and file refund claims to recover what you're owed.
  • Compliance review. Before an audit arrives, we review your POS configuration, return methodology, and record-keeping to close gaps and reduce your audit risk.
