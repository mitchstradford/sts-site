California Contractor Sales Tax Consulting

California construction sales tax involves materials, labor, fixtures, and contract structure — each with different tax treatment. We help contractors get it right and defend audits when they arise.

Why construction sales tax is uniquely complicated

California sales tax rules for contractors and construction companies are genuinely different from most other industries. Contractors are typically treated as consumers of the materials they incorporate into real property — not as retailers collecting tax from customers. But this framework has important exceptions, and the line between taxable and exempt transactions is blurry enough that even experienced contractors routinely make costly errors.

The CDTFA audits contractors regularly, and construction-related assessments tend to be large because material costs are high and errors often repeat across many projects.

The core framework: contractor as consumer

In most construction contracts, the contractor:

  • Pays sales tax (or use tax) when purchasing materials from suppliers
  • Does not collect sales tax from the customer on the construction contract price
  • Bears the sales tax obligation on incorporated materials — not the property owner

This is the starting point. But contract structure, the nature of what's being installed, and how billing is handled all create significant variations from this baseline.

Key taxability distinctions for contractors

  • Lump-sum vs. time-and-material contracts. In a lump-sum contract, you pay tax on material purchases and charge the customer a single untaxed price. In a time-and-material contract where materials are separately stated and billed at a markup, the CDTFA may treat you as a retailer of those materials — meaning you should have collected tax from the customer. Getting this wrong in either direction creates audit exposure.
  • Materials vs. fixtures. Items that lose their identity when incorporated into real property (materials) are taxed at purchase. Items that remain tangible personal property after installation (fixtures — think commercial kitchen equipment, HVAC units, certain appliances) are generally treated as retail sales to the customer, with tax owed on the selling price. Auditors and contractors frequently disagree on which category specific items fall into.
  • Subcontractor relationships. Labor payments to subcontractors are not taxable. Payments that include materials require analysis of who is providing materials and who is bearing the tax obligation. Proper documentation of each subcontract is essential.
  • Use tax on untaxed purchases. Materials purchased without paying sales tax — from out-of-state vendors or using an improper resale certificate — generate a use tax obligation that must be reported on your CDTFA return. Auditors request purchase records and compare them against reported use tax. Gaps produce assessments.

Common audit findings for contractors

  • Material purchases with no sales tax paid and no use tax reported
  • Fixtures treated as materials — failing to collect tax from the customer on the selling price
  • Time-and-material billings where tax wasn't collected on the material markup
  • Improper use of resale certificates on purchases that weren't resold
  • Subcontractor documentation insufficient to distinguish materials from labor
